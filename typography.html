On the Advantage of Nothing

15 March 2025  ·  1,100 words  ·  5 min read

Every design decision is also a decision about what not to show. The negative space between two lines of text carries as much meaning as the words themselves. When that space is wrong — too tight, too loose, inconsistent — the reader feels it before they can name it. The text becomes uncomfortable. The eye skips forward looking for an exit.

Spacing, in this sense, is not decoration. It is structure. It holds the reading experience together the way mortar holds bricks: invisibly, under compression, resisting collapse. The moment you remove it or add it arbitrarily, the whole thing starts to lean.

The secret of good typography is the space between the characters, not the characters themselves. — Adrian Frutiger

The baseline grid is the clearest expression of this idea. A fixed unit — eight pixels is the right choice for a screen — to which all vertical measurements must answer. Headings, body text, captions, code blocks, rules, padding: everything is a multiple of that unit. The result is not visible. That is the point. What the reader notices is that the page feels composed rather than assembled.

Eight pixels is the right unit because it is small enough to allow precise control and large enough that every spacing decision is deliberate rather than accidental. A twelve-pixel gap and a sixteen-pixel gap are meaningfully different. A twelve-pixel gap and a thirteen-pixel gap are not. The grid eliminates the thirteen.

The Typeface Problem

For years the typography conversation on the web has been dominated by custom fonts. A chosen typeface became a status signal: proof that someone cared enough to select it, pay for it, and load it. The logic is not wrong. A well-chosen typeface does communicate care. But the signal has a cost.

Every custom font is a network request. A layout-shift risk. A flash of unstyled text to be suppressed. A font file to be subsetted, versioned, hosted, and cached. None of these problems are insurmountable, but they are all real, and they are all paid for by the reader in milliseconds and shifted letters before the experience settles.

System fonts are the opposite argument. Georgia was designed by Matthew Carter in 1993 specifically for screen reading — high x-height, open apertures, generous spacing. Charter was designed by Carter for low-resolution printing and holds its quality at every screen resolution. Palatino, designed by Hermann Zapf in 1948, has been on every Mac for decades. The ui-serif keyword resolves to New York on Apple devices: a typeface designed for Apple's own editorial surfaces and tuned for retina displays.

None of these fonts require a network request. None of them cause layout shift. None of them have a file size. The reader already has them. They arrive at zero latency. The question is not whether they are good enough. The question is which one is doing the most work for this particular reading experience.

Choosing Without Loading

The panel in the lower right corner of this page switches the body typeface between six system font stacks without reloading anything. Georgia. Charter. Palatino. Optima. The modern ui-serif stack. The default system sans-serif. Each one produces a completely different reading feel from the same sixteen lines of CSS.

:root {
  --font-body: ui-serif, 'New York', Georgia, serif;
  --font-ui:   ui-sans-serif, system-ui, -apple-system, sans-serif;
}

body {
  font-family: var(--font-body);
  font-size: 18px;
  line-height: 32px; /* 4 × 8px baseline unit */
}

The key observation is not that one stack is correct. It is that the grid holds regardless of which stack is chosen. The rhythm survives the typeface change because the rhythm is in the spacing, not the letterforms. A well-composed page is well-composed in any font. A poorly composed page does not improve when you add a beautiful typeface to it.

Optima is the interesting case.1 Zapf designed it as a "serifless roman" — a sans-serif with the optical tension of an old-style serif, the strokes swelling and narrowing as if serifs were about to form but decided against it. It reads like a serif. It scales like a sans-serif. On Apple devices it is present and beautiful. On Windows it falls back to Candara, which is a reasonable relative. The combination produces something that feels neither corporate nor literary, but precise and considered.

The grid is the constant. The typeface is the variable. This page exists to find the right variable.

Notes

1. Optima is available by default on macOS and most Linux distributions. On Windows it falls back to Candara (included since Vista) or Noto Sans. The rendering differences between these fallbacks are significant enough to test on multiple platforms before committing to this stack. ↩
