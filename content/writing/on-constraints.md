# On Constraints

The assumption embedded in most tool selection is that more capability is better — that the right response to a hard problem is a more powerful instrument. This assumption is wrong often enough to be worth examining.

When a canvas is infinite, the first decision becomes load-bearing in a way it wouldn't be if the canvas had edges. Where do you start? What scale are you working at? What's the implied relationship between one mark and the next? These are questions the constraint answers before you ask it. The 8.5 × 11 inch page is not a limitation on the designer; it is the condition that makes design possible at all.

I notice this most clearly in software. The projects I've worked on that produced the cleanest code were not the ones with the most architectural freedom — they were the ones with the clearest constraints. A service that only does one thing. A schema that enforces a shape. A budget (time, compute, lines of code) that made the usual solution impossible and required finding a better one.

Constraints force a kind of attention that abundance discourages. When you cannot reach for the library, you must understand the problem. When you cannot add another field to the schema, you must understand the domain. When you cannot spend another week on the feature, you must understand what the feature actually is.

This is not an argument for artificial difficulty. Constraints imposed carelessly are just friction. The constraint that earns its place is the one derived from understanding — from knowing why the boundary is there and what it protects. A word count is a bad constraint if it's arbitrary; it's a good constraint if it encodes the attention span of the reader you're trying to reach.

The best constraints are also generative. The twelve-bar blues is a constraint. The sonnet is a constraint. The REST API contract is a constraint. Each one closes some doors and, in doing so, makes audible the music that was always latent in the remaining space. You find things inside a constraint that you would never have found without it, because the search space was too large and you would have stopped looking.

I try to build constraints in deliberately. Fixed type scales rather than arbitrary font sizes. A spacing system rather than ad hoc margins. A Makefile instead of a framework — not because Make is superior in every case, but because its limitations require understanding what the build actually does. The constraint is the documentation.

The work that lasts tends to have been made under pressure: of time, of medium, of form. The pressure is not incidental. It is, I think, the point.
