title: On Simplicity
description: Simplicity is not the absence of complexity. It is the residue left after the real complexity has been resolved.

---

Simplicity is not the absence of complexity. It is complexity that has been resolved, worked through until the residue is small enough to hold in one hand.

This distinction matters because the first kind of simplicity is easy to fake. Strip the interface down, hide the controls, remove the documentation. The system is no less complex. You have just moved the complexity somewhere less visible, usually into the user’s head. This is not simplicity. It is concealment.

Real simplicity requires understanding. You cannot simplify what you do not understand, because simplification is the act of finding the irreducible core and discarding the rest with confidence. The confidence is the hard part. It requires knowing what the rest is for, what would happen if you removed it, well enough to decide it is safe to cut.

In software this usually means spending more time than feels justified on the data model. The data model is where the complexity lives. A clean API over a confused schema is still a confused schema. The confusion will surface eventually, and it will surface at the worst possible time, which is when someone is trying to understand the code under pressure. A clean schema, one that reflects the actual shape of the domain, produces clean code almost automatically. The simplicity propagates upward.

I have come to think that simplicity is less a design goal than a signal. When a system feels complex, something is not yet understood. When it becomes simple, when the model snaps into place and the code contracts, that is evidence of understanding, not just good taste. The simplicity is earned, not applied.

This is why the injunction to keep it simple is almost useless as advice. It points at the destination without describing the path. The path is understanding. The simplicity follows.
